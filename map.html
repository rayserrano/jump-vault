The Map

Jump built the applications. Vault is the data platform beneath them.

This page shows the platform as it stands: nine shipped applications, four agents working inside one of them, and the clubs running on it today. Then it draws the data platform this site proposes underneath: one shared record of each fan that every application stands on and every client shares.

The Applications

These are the nine applications.

TICKETING

It sells single game, season, group, and premium seats, and it runs the resale marketplace.

DYNAMIC PRICING

It reprices seats as demand moves.

YIELD MANAGEMENT

It manages event inventory to maximize revenue.

FAN JOURNEY

It runs offers, segments, and campaigns across the fan lifecycle.

TEAM APPS & WEB

Every club gets its own branded app and site.

IDENTITY & WALLET

Each club’s fans get one login and one wallet.

LOYALTY & CREDITS

Fans earn and redeem credits and rewards.

INGRESS & POS

It scans fans into the venue and rings up sales inside it.

COMMERCE & CONCESSIONS

It sells merchandise and concessions in the same system as the tickets.

Since February 2026, four agents work inside the ticketing system of record: Pricing Manager, Inventory Manager, Campaign Generator, and Fan Intelligence. Denver Summit FC, the NC Courage, and the Timberwolves and Lynx run them today. Eric Caulfield, the Timberwolves Director of Ticketing, says work that used to take weeks now happens in minutes.
The Data Platform

The whole stack fits on one page.

The diagram reads from the bottom up. At the base, one layer holds a single shared record of each fan. From it, a data mart is stamped for each club. Above that sits the semantic layer, the one place every metric is defined, so every club and every agent reads the same numbers. The fast query layer serves answers to dashboards and apps, and the nine applications run on top.

The Clubs

Five clubs are live, and the next wave is signed.

NC Courage and North Carolina FC went live for the 2025 season, and Steve Malik, who owns both, called Jump the number one reason for their growth in 2025. The Minnesota Timberwolves came on for the 2025-26 season and the Lynx for 2026, sharing Target Center. Denver Summit FC opened its first season on the platform in 2026.

Three launches are stacked for 2027. The Seattle Sounders and Seattle Reign arrive as the first MLS clubs, moving off Ticketmaster at Lumen Field. Atlético Dallas debuts at the Cotton Bowl. Aberdeen FC opens the 2027/28 season as the first club outside the United States. Jump also lists the Athletics and Bucknell among its clients.

Two Ledgers

Every transaction posts to two ledgers.

When a fan buys a seat, the club books the revenue, and Jump collects its software fee plus a share of between 1 and 5 percent of the transaction. The incentives point the same way: Jump earns more only when its clubs do.

The data platform serves both ledgers at once. Anything that lifts a club’s fan revenue also lifts Jump’s take, and the cost of serving each club lands directly on Jump’s margin. The economics of the data platform are the economics of the company.

Three Tiers

Data warehousing becomes a product with three tiers.

No club should have to build a data engineering team to run its own warehouse. Even the ones that could staff one are better off spending that budget signing the next all-star. The data platform turns enterprise data warehousing into a product with three tiers. A club with no analysts gets a fully managed warehouse with governed metrics and finished reporting. A club with analysts gets self-serve access to the same governed semantic layer. An organization with real engineering capability gets governed data products published into its own stack, with contracts and lineage attached. One data platform serves all three tiers, and every tier reads the same canonical fan.
