Architecture

Nine layers hold the whole platform.

The rest of the site shows the architecture working. This page names it, one layer at a time, from the foundation to the open frontier. Every choice here is a settled pattern with its reasons on the table, and the two most forward-leaning layers are field reports rather than proposals.

View 2 · The Decisions

How value comes out: one governed truth, every path ending in a decision.

The stack exists to be consumed. This view spreads the top of it out: every consumer reaches the same governed source, through the tool that fits them, and each path ends not in a dashboard but in a decision. The one split that matters is whether the metric is defined once and governed, or improvised per team.

The distinction that carries this view is governance. Self-serve tools let each team define its own metrics, and those definitions drift until two dashboards disagree. The semantic layer defines each metric once and serves it everywhere, so an executive, an analyst, and an agent are all standing on the same number. Every consumer ends in a decision rather than a report, and because the ground under them is one governed truth, the decisions are consistent by construction.

View 3 · The Speed

Two temperatures: the analytical plane in hours, the operational plane in minutes.

Not every decision can wait for the nightly batch. The same platform serves a second, faster plane: an operational store kept current to the minute, feeding live dashboards, alerts, and webhooks. And when the serving plane needs true interactivity, Snowflake’s interactive tables and warehouses take it to sub-second: an always-warm serving tier inside the same governed platform, built for the high-concurrency reads a club portal generates, with no export to a separate engine. The question changes with the clock. Slow decisions ask how the season is going; fast ones ask what is happening right now.

Both planes read from the same resolved truth, so they never disagree; they differ only in latency and in the decision they serve. The analytical plane compounds knowledge over time. The operational plane catches the moment while it is still the moment. A platform that offers only one of them forces every question into the wrong clock.

Layer 1 · The Foundation

One integration layer beats fifty stitched marts.

Everything on this site has quietly been Inmon’s Corporate Information Factory wearing Jump clothes. The Map’s data platform diagram is the factory. The Onboard page is the integration layer working on camera. This page says the name out loud: one integration layer holds the canonical fan, client marts are derived above it, and the alternative of fifty club warehouses stitched together after the fact is how two-truths disease starts.

Layer 2 · The Model

Most models die of sprawl. This one absorbs change as data.

First, what this is not: nobody consumes this layer. Fans, clubs, and analysts read the club marts and the semantic layer above it. This is the foundation they stand on, kept rigorous here precisely so the surface they touch stays simple.

Most data models in the industry face the same slow death. A new business concept arrives, someone alters a table, and the model grows a column; a few years of that and an entity carries hundreds of flag columns, most of them storing the word no. Sprawl compounds, every change becomes a migration, and the platform calcifies until replacing it is cheaper than changing it. This is the ordinary way data platforms die.

The Universal Data Model is the compression of that whole problem. The model here is Silverston’s, and the pedigree runs decades deep. The macro pattern is a conglomeration of proven patterns, composed once. Classification is a pattern, a set of tables. Status is a pattern, a set of tables. So is item, so is role. Onboarding a new entity is not building tables; it is applying those patterns, which already exist. Each application leaves only a thin per-entity trace, and everything else it needs arrives as rows in structures that are already built.

One pattern makes the whole idea concrete. Take classification, the pattern that answers what a fan is. The sprawling model on the left grows a column for every label, which is why it drowns in flags storing no. The pattern holds the labels as rows instead, so a fan can carry any number of them and a brand-new label is a single insert. The season a club invents a new tier, no table changes.

The upside is a platform that does not compound its maintenance cost as it grows. New requirements become inserts instead of migrations. The same pattern absorbs permutations no one designed for: many labels at once, labels that change over time, whole new dimensions of them. Requirements shift the way business always shifts, and the model meets them with rows rather than a maintenance project, so the cost that normally compounds with every change never accrues. Club twelve onboards exactly the way club three did. The marts, the semantic layer, and the agents all stand on a model that does not shift under them. The pattern library and the rules for composing it are precisely what the implementation guides on the Mentoring page teach.

Layer 3 · The Access

The semantic layer is the constitution.

Every consumer on the data platform, human or agent, reads metrics from one governed semantic layer. A metric is defined once, in the open, with lineage, and no dashboard, model, or rule may redefine it. This is what makes agent-authored artifacts safe: a dashboard built on governed metrics cannot lie about revenue.

Layer 4 · The Intelligence

Models predict. Agents author. Rules execute.

The division of labor is strict. Models predict: propensity, uplift, and the scores on every fan row. Agents author: they read the warehouse, write rules, build assets, and retire what stops working. Rules execute: every high-volume decision runs deterministically. Nothing with a per-transaction cost sits inside a language model loop.

Layer 5 · The Serving

The warehouse thinks. The serving layer answers.

Agents changed the shape of the query load. Club analysts ran bursty dashboards where seconds were fine. Embedded analytics for every club, fan-facing surfaces, and agents in reasoning loops produce thousands of small queries around the clock, and a slow answer stalls an agent mid-thought. Pointing that workload at the thinking tier buys a warehouse that never sleeps: warehouse-seconds economics applied to a workload that wants cost-per-query economics. This tradeoff has been run in production, and the page carries the judgment.

The semantic layer spans the temperatures. The serving store materializes governed metrics and never redefines them, and data flows one way: integration, then marts, then materialize, then serve. A serving copy outside the warehouse makes freshness and consistency governed properties too; ignore that and you reinvent the two-truths disease the platform exists to cure.

Whether one platform now fills both analytical temperatures or two engines share one contract is a criteria decision: concurrency, latency, cost per query, operations burden, and semantic-layer integration. The warehouse vendors moved into interactive serving this year, and the dedicated engines are not standing still. The architecture holds either way, and the choice is margin engineering: platform cost per client lands directly on the Path to Black.

Layer 6 · The Execution

The rules engine decides at zero token cost.

Rules engines are a category with a graveyard, and this page says so. They died because humans could not maintain thousands of rules, and sprawl rotted every deployment. Agents fix the exact failure that killed the category: an agent observes outcomes through the warehouse, retires stale rules, proposes refinements, and backtests before anything deploys. And rules fix what stalls agents, because tokens cannot price a per-transaction decision, while a compiled rule decides at a marginal cost near zero. AI makes rules possible. Rules make AI possible at scale.

The loop is the flywheel’s inner gearbox: the agent analyzes the warehouse, proposes a rule, the backtest scores it, a human approves inside guardrails, the engine deploys, telemetry flows back, and the agent refines. Every decision traces to a rule version: inspectable, reversible, and auditable, which matters more the day the first European club goes live and automated decisions meet European regulation. This pattern is also a field report: agents composing rules against a governed warehouse is a system that has run in production, not a concept from a slide.

Jump’s own launch copy describes the Pricing Manager modeling scenarios and pushing pricing changes across events in minutes. That is rule authorship and deployment, already shipped. Five of the nine applications are rules domains wearing product clothes: pricing, yield, journey triggers, loyalty accrual, and ingress entitlements. Vault proposes one governed decision layer instead of five embedded ones. The general law follows: the agent is the composer and the analytics estate is the composition. Models, rules, and BI assets are all authored expensively in tokens, executed at near-zero cost, and governed by the same semantic constitution.

Layer 7 · The Boundary

Centralize the integration. Federate the consumption.

Centralization and mesh stop being rivals when each takes the half it is good at. The integration stays centralized: one canonical fan, one place identity resolves. The consumption federates: capable organizations receive governed data products, with contracts and lineage attached, published into their own stacks. That is the third DWaaS tier from the Map, and it is the boundary where the platform ends on purpose.

Layer 8 · The Identity

The whole person lives in the integration layer, and each club owns its truth.

The identity ontology follows the layering. The integration layer holds the resolved party: a fan, an account, an organization, whichever entity is hardest to join in a given deployment, with every source key mapped, every relationship typed. Each club’s mart carries only that club’s truth. Progressive profiling, customer 360, and lifetime value are all views stamped from the same resolved party, which is why they agree with each other, and why the fan graph option on the Bench page costs nothing to keep open.

Because tenancy lives on the relationship and not on the fan, the model never assumes what kind of tenant it serves. A club and a touring act look the same to it: fans, transactions, and a lifecycle to own. The UDM would handle music fans exactly as it handles sports fans, with the same resolved person, the same governed marts, for whenever someone decides to make them Jump.

The same shared person makes something else possible. Because every club reads the same resolved party, the platform can recognize when non-competing clubs, a basketball club and a football club in one market, are serving the same supporter. Each still owns its own relationship with her; what a network of clubs chooses to build on that recognition, under governance, is the opportunity that sits above the platform.

Layer 9 · The Horizon

The frontier is attention.

Every layer above answers what is true, faster and cheaper each layer down. The open question is what deserves attention.

activation = base_level + contextual_boost + novelty_bonus

The shape comes from cognitive science, Baars on the global workspace, Oberauer on working memory, and Anderson on activation, and it runs in the rules engine at zero token cost, with the language model outside the attention loop.
