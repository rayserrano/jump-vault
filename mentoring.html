Mentoring

The platform is a team before it is a system.

The Engineering page showed the code. This page holds the guides that teach it, the method that produced those guides, and the loop that keeps the guides and the engineers sharpening each other. Jump wrote people development into this role, and this page treats it the way the rest of the site treats architecture: as a discipline with a method, evidence, and a feedback loop.

The Method

Engineers learn to recognize patterns first.

The method assumes strong engineers. The patterns on this stack are uncommon, and the point is to move an experienced hire from new to the pattern to fluent in the least time their calendar allows. DIGEST stands for Decomposed Integration for Genuine Skill Transfer. It is the method behind every guide on this page, compiled over years of mentoring technical and business teams. The core rule is ordering: material is decomposed so that no piece references anything that has not been taught yet, and each piece moves an engineer from recognizing a pattern to applying it to explaining it fluently.

The Lineage

The method extends a published discipline.

BEAM, from Corr and Stagnitto’s Agile Data Warehouse Design, taught that data models are gathered collaboratively and by example, around the business events people can actually describe. DIGEST applies the same instinct one layer up, to skill transfer: teach by worked example on real material, in the order a learner can absorb it.

The Method, Applied

The platform is finished when the team can run it without its builder.

A strong engineer leaves behind working systems. A platform leader leaves behind a team that can extend them. This section holds implementation guides written for the engineers who will live in the stack, and every guide follows the same shape: prerequisites, the steps with a worked example beside each one, the reason behind each step, validation checks with expected results, and the mistakes to expect. The shape is the point. A guide built this way keeps the platform teachable rather than tribal, and it is how standards survive a growing team.

Documentation on this stack does not slow the work it teaches. The platform lead rules the method and the standard, and a digital intelligence drafts each guide at machine speed. Each guide is also written for two readers at once: the engineer, and the engineer’s own digital collaborator, which holds the guide in context and answers against it. No model arrives with this stack in its weights; the guides are how a digital collaborator learns it. The result is that the engineer digests the material at conversation speed instead of page speed.

The repository itself begins as a teaching artifact. The platform lead builds its first version, both to put real work in motion on day one and to leave behind runnable examples of every primary pattern: in dbt, in Snowflake, on the Universal Data Model, and in the semantic layer. The team inherits a codebase that already demonstrates its own standards.

Three parts of the skeleton carry most of the weight. The scope section draws boundaries, and the sharpest one is what is intentionally not done, because knowing where a task ends is what keeps club work out of the shared layers. The tests section makes the layers a gate rather than a suggestion. And the rollback section treats reversal as a designed path, which is what makes engineers willing to ship. ## 1.3 What is intentionally not done

This guide does not touch identity resolution. The mart
consumes resolved parties from the integration layer; it
never creates or merges them. If onboarding surfaces fans
the platform cannot resolve, that is integration-layer work
and it has its own guide. Doing it here would put club-scoped
logic inside the shared layer, and the whole tenancy model
depends on that line staying where it is.

-- Current and lapsed memberships for the club being
-- onboarded. Grain assumption: one membership row per
-- (fan_id, club_id). If the source carries renewals as new

## 6.3 Validation check three: relationship edge count

SELECT COUNT(*) AS edge_count
FROM INTEGRATION.PARTY_RELATIONSHIP pr
INNER JOIN INTEGRATION.PARTY_ROLE r
    ON pr.to_party_role_id = r.party_role_id
INNER JOIN INTEGRATION.PARTY p
    ON r.party_id = p.party_id
WHERE p.party_code = 'TIX|CLUB|77';

Expected: within two percent of the club's source
membership count (illustrative: 41,900 to 42,700 against a
42,310-row extract). A count far below means resolution is
dropping fans. A count above means the uniqueness test in
section 4.1 has a gap. Either way, stop before the mart
build and find out which.

The version line at the top is the part that matters most. Guides ship as v1, and the implementing engineer audits them before executing. The audit comes back as findings, and v2 answers every finding by name in its own header. Open questions get the same routing, split between what is already resolved and what the engineer can answer from the repo and the source data. This is practiced rather than invented: the delivery history behind this page carries exactly these headers. A guide the engineer can audit and change is the difference between a team that executes instructions and a team that owns the platform, and the version history is the receipt.

Guide 2, Adding a Metric to the Semantic Layer, follows the same shape at smaller scope: from the metric’s business definition, through its declaration in code, to the proof that every consumer, dashboard or agent, now returns the same number.

The Audit Loop

Review is what makes the guides version.

The audit points in both directions: the guide answers to the engineers who run it, and the standard earns its authority by surviving their findings. Most of the reviewing is itself done by digital intelligences, with the engineer in governance mode: one session audits another session’s work, a digital second opinion that catches hallucination without giving up machine speed. Shipped work gets audited, and the findings feed the guides. Guide 1 above carries its v2 status header because the implementing engineer’s v1 audit came back with findings, and the header answers each one by name. An engineer’s growth shows up the same way, as the measurable distance between their v1 and their v2.
