The Premise

One product suite, nine applications. Let’s add a unified data platform and make the revenue compound.

Jump’s applications already earn the money: ticketing, commerce, pricing, the team apps, and the agents launched in 2026. A unified data platform claims none of that revenue for itself. It sits underneath, and it’s what lets every application run at full speed on every client. The gain it’s measured by is the revenue that would not have arrived otherwise: incremental fan revenue per client. And that gain compounds, because every transaction makes the fan record richer, a richer record makes the next offer land better, and this year’s wins raise next year’s starting line. The rest of this page lays it out as three engines around one number.

The Three Engines

Three engines, each feeding a revenue line.

VAULT YIELD

Raise spend per fan, on every client

AI agents run on a governed semantic layer and hunt revenue in fan data: the season holder who never bought merch, the merch buyer who never upgraded, the group-buy organizer who should be seeing suite offers. The same agents run bundling analysis across ticket, merch, and concession, and they find segments where engagement outruns monetization.

The mechanics are one governed metric truth per client mart and agents that query the system of record. Every incremental fan dollar sends a share to Jump’s own revenue. The 2026 agent launch proved the pattern once. Yield repeats it on every client.

VAULT ONBOARD

Turn client acquisition into manufacturing

Today every new club is a bespoke integration, so onboarding capacity throttles the growth story. The fix is an integration layer on a canonical model. Under that layer, onboarding is a mapping exercise: ticketing, commerce, app, and historical CRM map into one universal model, and the client mart is stamped out on top.

The product metric is time-to-live-revenue per client. With multiple go-lives stacking in 2027, this engine is the difference between a queue and a production line.

From fragmented to compounding
Fragmented Phase

Every application flew from its own base.

The story runs in five pictures. Each of the deeper pages zooms into one of them.

Four systems, one per category, each walled off from the rest — four aircraft with nowhere shared to fly from. The fan is the same person in all four, but each system filed her under a different identifier, and none can see the others. The severed links in crimson are the cross-sell that never happens.

Every organization has an integration challenge. The fan is its most common face and the one drawn here; the platform anchors whichever one is hardest in your world.

Connected Phase

The applications are connected. The fan is not.

Application integration is real, and it is the achievement: the applications call each other with events, and the experience feels like one system. Data integration is a different problem. Each application still keeps its own record of the fan, and nothing in an event backbone can say that three of those records are one person. This is how every event-connected platform looks at the data layer. All records shown are illustrative.

Unified Phase

The data platform resolves every fan to one record.

The applications earn as one coordinated unit, and the intelligence works beside them. Beneath them sits the data platform with its three plain promises: every metric has one definition, every club has its own mart, and every fan resolves to one record. Jessica from the last picture is one person here. The stream of transactions feeds it all, as it happens. The full anatomy, in engineering terms, lives on the Map and Architecture pages.

Owned Phase

Every club owns its data, stamped from one source.

Own your fans, your data, your revenue is the promise, and the mart is how the data platform keeps it: every club gets its own, wholly owned — the Timberwolves, NC Courage, the Sounders, with Aberdeen’s mart under construction for the 2027/28 debut. The detail that matters is the source. All of them are stamped from the one unified data platform, so ownership never forks the truth. The three service tiers live on the Map.

Yield lifts what fans spend, Onboard adds clubs faster, and Bench sells the view across them. Every turn enriches the data platform, and a richer platform raises the next turn. The loop feeds one number, and the chart below prices it.

One Asset, Two Markets

The sports platform quietly opens a second market.

Jump’s business is sports, and that is where this platform earns its keep. But the test of platform leverage is whether the same fixed cost earns across more than one market. This one does. The platform Jump builds so a club can own its fans is, unchanged, the platform that lets a touring artist own theirs. The same incumbent to displace, the same data to reclaim, in a larger and more international market. The hard part is built once and earns in both, because the data model does not care which kind of fan it holds.

And often it is the very same fan: the arena that hosts the game hosts the concert. Each club owns its own fans, but Jump, beneath all of them, is the only party that sees the whole graph — which fans fill which venues, across every market on the platform. That network view is leverage no single club and no legacy vendor can match, and it grows with every club added. It is the aggregator position the incumbents built their empires on, arriving here as a byproduct of getting the data platform right. None of this asks Jump to change what it is. It is the same fan in the same building — the game is one show, the concert is another. Jump already owns that fan’s experience; the data platform is simply what lets it own every show, not just the ones with a scoreboard.

The North Star
IFR/C: Incremental Fan Revenue per Client

How many incremental fan dollars did the platform generate, per client, this quarter? Yield raises it. Onboard multiplies how many clients count toward it. Bench adds margin to it. It is one number the board, the sales deck, and the roadmap can all share, and it anchors the roadmap against feature creep: if an item does not move IFR/C, it waits.
