The Client Velocity Engine

Onboard turns client acquisition into manufacturing.

Client Velocity is one of the platform’s three engines; Onboard is its first feature, and it turns the lever that pulls breakeven closer. Today every new club is a bespoke integration, and onboarding capacity throttles the growth story. Three launches are already stacked for 2027, with the first international club behind them. Onboard replaces the bespoke project with a factory: one canonical model to map into, and a client mart stamped out on top. All data on this page is illustrative.

The Problem

Every club arrives as incompatible systems.

The same fan carries three identities with zero joins, multiplied by every source and every club.

The Founding Decision

One fan record. Each club owns only its relationship to that fan.

The platform keeps one record per fan. The club is a party too, so the fan and the club are joined by a relationship rather than by storing a club name on the fan’s data. A fan of two clubs stays one fan, with a separate relationship to each, and that is what keeps every club’s data its own. The model below shows the shape.

Joining the club by a relationship, instead of storing it on the data, keeps every club’s data separate now and keeps one fan account across clubs possible later, if Jump ever chooses it.

The Intake Discipline

The mess stops at the door.

Real onboarding is rarely tidy. A new club arrives needed yesterday, with source systems that were never designed to leave home, and data quality is usually the first casualty. The pattern that contains it: all custom work is quarantined into one craft, mapping the club’s sources to a standard set of onboarding tables. From the tables inward, the ride is automated and governed. The chaos gets a room; the platform never inherits it. That mapping craft is also a team: onboarding engineers whose whole discipline is source-to-table mapping and the staging build.

The Factory

Every client mart comes off the same press.

Once a club’s sources map into the model, the mart is generated: the same dimensional shapes, the same governed metrics, and only the mapping is client-specific. The dashed box in the Map’s data platform diagram is this press waiting for its next job.

The Metric

Time-to-live-revenue is the product metric.

Onboard is measured in the time from contract to the first revenue-bearing transaction on the platform. It is also the chart’s velocity multiplier wearing its working clothes: every week cut from onboarding raises the number of clubs the factory ships in a year, which is exactly the lever the Path to Black turns when Onboard switches on. With the Sounders, the Reign, Atlético Dallas, and Aberdeen stacked for 2027, this engine is the difference between a queue and a production line. Every week cut from onboarding starts the revenue share earlier, and both ledgers feel it.
