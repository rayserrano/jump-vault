The Onboard Engine

Onboard turns client acquisition into manufacturing.

Today every new club is a bespoke integration, and onboarding capacity throttles the growth story. Three launches are already stacked for 2027, with the first international club behind them. Onboard replaces the bespoke project with a factory: one canonical model to map into, and a client mart stamped out on top. All data on this page is illustrative.

The Problem

Every club arrives as incompatible systems.

The same fan carries three identities with zero joins, multiplied by every source and every club.

The Founding Decision

Tenancy lives on the relationship edge.

The integration layer resolves all three identities to one canonical person, and then it makes the decision that everything else inherits: the person carries no tenant. The club lives on the relationship, so a fan of two Jump clubs is one person with two edges, each fully isolated inside its club’s walls.

Tenancy on the relationship keeps every club fully isolated today, and it is the only design that leaves a cross-property fan graph possible tomorrow. Whether fans ever get one account across clubs is a product decision, and this model is the one that refuses to foreclose it.

The Factory

Every client mart comes off the same press.

Once a club’s sources map into the model, the mart is generated: the same dimensional shapes, the same governed metrics, and only the mapping is client-specific. The dashed box in the Map’s data platform diagram is this press waiting for its next job.

The Metric

Time-to-live-revenue is the product metric.

Onboard is measured in the time from contract to the first revenue-bearing transaction on the platform. With the Sounders, the Reign, Atlético Dallas, and Aberdeen stacked for 2027, this engine is the difference between a queue and a production line. Every week cut from onboarding starts the revenue share earlier, and both ledgers feel it.
